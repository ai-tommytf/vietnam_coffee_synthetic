# Vietnam’s Coffee Industry: Key Facts, Yields, and Climate Impacts

Vietnam as a Global Coffee Powerhouse

Vietnam is the world’s second-largest coffee producer (after Brazil) and by far the largest producer of robusta coffee ￼. Coffee is a crucial export commodity for Vietnam – in 2023, coffee exports earned a record $4.24 billion for the country ￼. Vietnam contributes roughly 20% of the world’s coffee supply (and around 40% of global robusta supply) ￼, underscoring the country’s importance in the international coffee market. This dominance in robusta (a typically stronger, more caffeinated coffee variety) makes Vietnam a key player influencing global coffee prices, especially for instant coffee and espresso blends that often use robusta.

Economically, coffee has transformed Vietnam’s agriculture over the past few decades. The industry grew from only about 1.5% of world output in 1990 to making Vietnam the number two producer worldwide today ￼. Millions of smallholder farmers in Vietnam depend on coffee for their livelihoods, and the sector’s growth has uplifted rural incomes (though it has also introduced volatility and debt risks during market downturns). Almost 99% of Vietnam’s coffee output is exported as raw beans (mainly to the EU, U.S., and other Asian markets), since domestic consumption is relatively modest (around 10% of production) ￼. However, domestic coffee drinking is on the rise, and initiatives are in place to increase local consumption as well ￼. Overall, coffee remains a cornerstone of Vietnam’s agricultural exports and rural economy.

Main Coffee Regions and Varieties in Vietnam

Coffee cultivation in Vietnam is heavily concentrated in the Central Highlands region. This plateau region (Tây Nguyên) – which includes provinces like Đắk Lắk, Lâm Đồng, Đắk Nông, Gia Lai, and Kon Tum – accounts for about 90% of the nation’s coffee output and 92% of the planted area ￼. The Central Highlands provide ideal growing conditions: moderate elevation, rich volcanic “red basalt” soils, and distinct wet/dry seasons that suit robusta coffee particularly well ￼. Đắk Lắk province (around Buôn Ma Thuột city) is the single largest coffee-producing province. Other significant areas include Lâm Đồng (home to the city of Đà Lạt) and Gia Lai. In these highland areas, coffee is a backbone of the local economy and landscape – one can find vast plantations and smallholder plots blanketing the hills.

Robusta vs. Arabica: Vietnam is overwhelmingly a robusta coffee producer. Roughly 96–97% of Vietnam’s coffee output is Coffea canephora (robusta) ￼ ￼, a hardy variety known for its high caffeine and typically used in instant coffee and espresso blends. Robusta thrives in Vietnam’s climate at 500–800 m elevations. In contrast, arabica coffee (Coffea arabica) makes up only around 3–4% of production ￼ ￼. Arabica requires higher elevations and cooler temperatures; thus, it is grown in only a few zones. The primary arabica-growing area is southern Lâm Đồng province (around Cầu Đất, Đà Lạt) where elevations of 1,500–1,600 m and misty, cool weather yield higher-quality arabica beans ￼. Smaller arabica initiatives exist in some northern highlands (e.g. Sơn La province, ~1,000 m altitude) and central provinces like Quảng Trị (Khe Sanh area), but these remain limited niches ￼ ￼. In summary, Vietnam’s coffee sector is dominated by robusta in the Central Highlands, with arabica playing a minor specialty role in a few high-altitude pockets.

Vietnam’s Coffee Yields and Production Levels

One standout feature of Vietnamese coffee farming is its high yield. Vietnamese coffee farms are highly productive, thanks to intensive cultivation techniques (irrigation, fertilization, high-density planting) and favorable growing conditions. Average yields in Vietnam are around 2.7–2.9 metric tons of green coffee per hectare in recent years ￼ ￼. For example, in 2020 Vietnam’s average yield was reported at 2.73 tons/ha, which is over three times the world’s average yield for coffee ￼. Nationally, yields tend to hover in the 2.5–3.0 ton/ha range, and under good practices yields can reach up to ~3.5–3.7 ton/ha on some farms ￼. By comparison, the global average yield (across all countries and varieties) is roughly on the order of 0.8 ton/ha, so Vietnam’s productivity is exceptionally high.

This high yield means Vietnam produces a large volume of coffee from a modest land area (about 700,000+ hectares of coffee). In recent crop years, Vietnam’s total production has been in the range of 1.5–1.8 million metric tons of coffee annually (equivalent to ~25–30 million 60-kg bags). For instance, the 2020 output was estimated at 1.74 million tonnes ￼, and recent seasons have fluctuated around 27–30 million bags of production ￼. Vietnam’s robusta yields are among the highest in the world, reflecting the country’s efficient agricultural practices. However, maintaining these yields can be challenging as trees age and require replanting – Vietnam’s government and farmers have ongoing programs to replace old coffee trees and even experiment with crop diversification (some farmers intercropping or switching part of their land to durian, avocado, etc., seeking higher profits) ￼. Still, coffee yields per hectare in Vietnam remain in the top tier globally, which has solidified the country’s status as a coffee exporting giant ￼.

Climate Impacts: Floods, Droughts, and Weather Risks

Vietnam’s coffee regions face significant climate and weather-related risks, especially from swings between drought and heavy rainfall. The Central Highlands have a tropical monsoon climate with a pronounced dry season (roughly December–April) and a rainy season (May–November). Drought in the dry season is a perennial concern: if the rains arrive late or are insufficient, water shortages can stress the coffee trees. On the other hand, during the wet season, intense rains and floods can occur, sometimes causing landslides or waterlogging in farms. In recent years, climate variability (potentially exacerbated by climate change) has made these extremes more frequent and severe ￼ ￼.

Droughts: Severe droughts hit the coffee belt during strong El Niño years. A notable example was the 2015–2016 drought, one of the worst on record, which drastically reduced water availability. In 2016, Vietnam’s coffee production fell by roughly 20% due to the drought’s impact on yields ￼. Reservoirs and rivers ran low, and thousands of hectares of coffee either withered or had to be abandoned. In total, the 2014–2016 El Niño drought damaged over 56,000 ha of coffee in the Central Highlands (with around 4,400 ha completely lost) ￼. More recently, 2023–2024 brought another severe drought (associated with a new El Niño event): 2024 saw the most intense drought in a decade, drying up rivers and affecting over 7,000 ha of coffee fields (many trees were scorched or stunted) ￼. Farmers had to drill wells and irrigate with groundwater at high cost, and even so, the 2023/24 coffee crop is estimated to have dropped by nearly 20% from normal levels ￼ ￼. Drought primarily hurts coffee by causing flowering failures and cherry drop – extended hot, dry conditions in the flowering period lead to poor fruit set, and any subsequent rains can be “too little, too late” or cause additional stress. Studies estimate that ongoing patterns of prolonged dry spells and erratic rain could cut Vietnamese coffee yields by ~15% on average, due to disrupted flowering and tree stress ￼. Drought has therefore become a major challenge, requiring adaptation measures (like improved irrigation, drought-resistant varietals, or shading techniques) to maintain yields.

Floods and Heavy Rain: Paradoxically, while drought is a threat in one season, flooding and excessive rainfall pose risks in the other. Vietnam as a whole is prone to tropical storms – the country is affected by 8–10 typhoons or tropical storms each year on average, especially hitting the coast ￼. The Central Highlands are inland and somewhat shielded from direct typhoon landfalls; however, dying storms or monsoon systems can still dump torrential rains on these highlands. Flash floods and landslides have become frequent occurrences in coffee provinces during the peak rainy months ￼. For instance, a historic flood in August 2019 swept across all five Central Highland provinces, causing deadly landslides and inundating farms – at least 10 people died, 3,700+ houses were flooded, and over 8,300 ha of crops (including coffee and rice) were damaged in that event ￼. Again in late 2021, intense rains led to floods in Đắk Lắk province that killed 19 people and submerged thousands of hectares of agriculture ￼. These floods can directly damage coffee trees (through waterlogging or soil erosion) and also delay harvesting if roads are cut off or fields are too muddy.

Even very recently, Vietnam’s coffee heartland has seen extreme rainfall. In November 2025, parts of Đắk Lắk were deluged by nearly 1,800 mm of rain in a single week, causing severe floods ￼. This unprecedented downpour (fed by a La Niña weather pattern) delayed the coffee harvest in the nation’s top coffee province and threatened to dent the output of that season ￼. Such excessive rain during harvest time causes ripe coffee cherries to rot or fall off the trees and makes it difficult to sun-dry the beans, potentially reducing both yield and quality. In general, heavy rains following drought are especially problematic – farmers report that after a drought, sudden heavy rainfall can cause cherries to drop en masse and leach nutrients from the soil ￼ ￼.

Overall, the frequency of floods and other extreme weather in Vietnam’s coffee regions appears to be rising. Government reports note that floods, flash floods, and landslides now occur regularly each rainy season in the Central Highlands, and their scale and damage have been increasing in the last decade ￼ ￼. Climate models project continued volatility, meaning Vietnamese coffee farmers must increasingly manage “boom and bust” weather cycles – one year might bring a bumper crop under perfect weather, and the next year a storm or drought could cut production sharply. This climate risk is a critical factor in Vietnam’s coffee outlook.

Regardless of specific metrics, both government and growers are realizing the need for climate adaptation: better water management (irrigation and drainage), shade trees to buffer extreme heat, and possibly shifting some production to higher elevations less prone to flooding. Initiatives like “climate-smart coffee” pilot programs are now underway in provinces like Gia Lai ￼, aiming to equip farmers with tools to cope with drought/flood cycles and sustain yields.

Vietnamese Coffee Production and Climate Hazard Overview

Key Factors Driving Vietnam’s Coffee Production

Vietnam is the world’s second-largest coffee producer (after Brazil) and by far the largest producer of robusta coffee ￼. Several key factors have enabled this success. First, ideal agro-climatic conditions in the Central Highlands (provinces like Dak Lak, Lam Dong, Gia Lai, etc.) provide a perfect environment for coffee. This region offers high elevations (often 500–1500 m), rich volcanic basalt soils, and a distinct tropical monsoon climate with separate wet and dry seasons – conditions well-suited for high-yield robusta cultivation ￼. The pronounced rainy season each year supports vegetative growth, while a drier period is crucial for flowering and fruit development, creating an optimal growth cycle for coffee plants. These natural advantages, combined with resilient robusta varieties, make it possible to achieve high yields. Today robusta accounts for about 95% of Vietnam’s coffee output, with over 600,000 hectares under coffee cultivation producing around 30 million bags annually ￼ ￼.

Second, economic and historical factors have driven expansion. The coffee sector grew rapidly after market reforms in the late 1980s (Đổi Mới) allowed private farming. Farmers aggressively expanded coffee farms across the Central Highlands’ fertile lands. As a result, Vietnam’s coffee area and output skyrocketed – from roughly 20,000 ha in 1975 to over 600,000 ha today ￼. Government support, investments in modern processing, and high global coffee prices in recent years have further encouraged production ￼ ￼. In summary, favorable climate and soil conditions (ample rainfall, warm temperatures, rich soil), geography (upland elevation), and policy-driven agricultural expansion are the key drivers behind Vietnam’s dominant coffee industry.

Climate Influences on Coffee Yield Variability

While Vietnam’s climate is generally favorable, coffee production is highly sensitive to year-to-year weather variability. Studies show that robusta yields can fluctuate significantly based on rainfall patterns and other weather factors ￼. Two critical periods have been identified: (1) a longer rainy season in the prior year tends to boost the next harvest by promoting vegetative growth (more branches and fruiting nodes), and (2) adequate rainfall during the bean formation stage (after flowering) is vital – if conditions are too dry at that time, bean development suffers, reducing yields ￼. In other words, both excessive drought and excessive rain at the wrong times can negatively impact coffee output. One analysis found that in key provinces like Dak Lak and Lam Dong, up to 36% of the variation in annual robusta yields could be explained by weather anomalies ￼. This underscores that Vietnamese coffee is heavily weather-dependent.

Temperature is another factor: robusta can tolerate heat better than arabica, but there are limits. Research suggests that for robusta in Vietnam, every 1°C increase above an optimal range (~16–24°C minimum/maximum) can lead to roughly a 14% yield decline ￼. Prolonged high temperatures speed up bean ripening and stress the plants, ultimately lowering quality and productivity. Thus, steady moderate temperatures with no extremes are ideal for coffee.

Flood Risk in Vietnam’s Coffee Regions

Vietnam as a whole is highly prone to floods and tropical storms due to its monsoonal climate and long coastline. On average, the country is struck by numerous typhoons and intense rain events each year, especially from late summer into autumn ￼ ￼. Central Vietnam (the mid-coastal provinces and nearby highlands) often experiences the brunt of these late-year storms. In fact, floods account for an estimated 97% of Vietnam’s average annual disaster economic losses, illustrating their outsized impact ￼. Historically, catastrophic floods have occurred periodically – for example, a succession of typhoons in late 2020 caused severe inundation in central Vietnam, resulting in 192 deaths, tens of thousands of homes submerged, and ~50,000 ha of crops destroyed ￼. Likewise, records were broken in October 2025 when the heritage city of Huế received over 1 meter of rainfall in 24 hours, the highest daily total ever observed in Vietnam ￼. Such extreme downpours led to widespread flooding of cities and river basins. Climate data indicate these heavy rainfall events have become more intense, with scientists warning that warming temperatures are making storms “increasingly powerful” ￼.

The Central Highlands coffee belt is somewhat sheltered from direct coastal typhoons, but it is not immune to flooding. Coffee farms are typically on rolling plateaus, so they avoid the routine river floods that plague low-lying deltas. However, torrential rains can still trigger dangerous flash floods and landslides in this region. In November 2025, for instance, Vietnam’s central highlands were drenched by an unusually prolonged rain episode. Parts of Dak Lak province (the heart of robusta production) saw up to 1,792 mm of rain in just a few weeks – an extraordinary amount, delivered by a series of tropical storms and a La Niña-strengthened monsoon ￼. This deluge led to floodwaters submerging farms and villages, landslides cutting off roads, and dozens of fatalities nationally ￼. The coffee industry was hit hard: as of mid-November 2025, many low-lying coffee farms in Dak Lak were deep underwater, just as the harvest was beginning ￼. Farmers had to halt picking in about 10–15% of plantations, and drying yards were drenched, raising concerns of mold developing on the beans ￼. Local traders reported that heavy rains were “slowing down the harvest” and preventing beans from sun-drying properly ￼. In nearby Lam Dong province, thousands of coffee trees had their roots washed out by floodwaters, which will likely reduce next season’s yields due to plant damage ￼. This event highlighted that even upland crop areas face serious flood risk when rainfall is extreme. In general, the coffee regions experience their rainy season from May to October; if those rains are unusually intense or extend longer than normal, flooding and waterlogging can occur, especially in valleys and along streams.

It’s important to note that Vietnam’s disaster authorities are well aware of these risks. The government’s national disaster strategy identifies floods and landslides among the top hazards to mitigate ￼ ￼. Historically, most flood disasters (and associated casualties) have hit the densely populated river deltas and coastal areas ￼. But recent events show that the frequency of extreme rainfall in the highlands may be increasing, potentially putting coffee areas in harm’s way more often. Overall, flood risk in coffee-growing regions is tied to unusual storm tracks or monsoon surges that bring heavy inland rainfall. When those occur, the consequences can be severe for coffee cultivation – from physical crop damage and soil erosion to delayed harvests and quality losses.

Other Weather Hazards Affecting Coffee Production

Aside from floods, Vietnamese coffee producers must contend with several other climate-related hazards that drive year-to-year variability:
	•	Drought and Water Stress:  Drought is the flip side of Vietnam’s climate volatility. The Central Highlands have pronounced dry seasons, and in some years rainfall can be far below normal. A severe drought will stress coffee trees, reduce cherry formation, and can sharply cut yields. For example, prolonged drought conditions in 2023 contributed to an over 10% drop in Vietnam’s coffee output compared to the previous year ￼. Historically, major droughts in 2015–2016 and 2019–2020 hit the Central Highlands and south-central coast hard, with 2015–16 causing an estimated $1.5 billion in agricultural losses across Vietnam ￼ ￼. In coffee farms, insufficient soil moisture during the flowering and fruiting stages leads to fewer cherries and smaller bean size. Farmers do irrigate in some areas, but water shortages can make irrigation impossible in extreme dry spells. Thus, El Niño years or any climate pattern that reduces wet-season rains pose a significant hazard for coffee production.
	•	Storms and High Winds: Tropical cyclones (typhoons or tropical storms) typically weaken by the time they reach the Central Highlands, but they can still bring strong winds and heavy downpours. High winds can strip coffee cherries off branches or even topple shade trees. Early November 2025 saw Typhoon Kalmaegi brush the coffee belt with intense gusts and rain – most farms escaped severe wind damage, but some experienced cherry drop (premature fruit fall) due to the storm ￼. More commonly, storms manifest as intense rain makers in the highlands (leading back to the flood risk discussed above). However, farmers remain vigilant during the late-season harvest period (Oct–Dec) in case of any typhoon-driven weather.
	•	Heat and Unusual Temperature Extremes: While not as dramatic as floods or droughts, temperature trends are a creeping hazard. Coffee thrives in a fairly narrow temperature range. In recent years, Vietnam has seen record-high temperatures and heatwaves. Excessive heat can desiccate the soil and interfere with coffee blossoming. Extended hot periods (especially if coinciding with drought) exacerbate water stress on the trees. Climate change projections indicate that mean temperatures in Vietnam are rising, which could gradually push some coffee areas out of their optimal climate zone. One study noted that robusta yields decline sharply once maximum temperatures regularly exceed ~25 °C ￼ ￼. Thus, climate warming is a long-term threat, potentially requiring shifts to higher-altitude cultivation or more heat-tolerant varieties in the future.
	•	Pests and Diseases: Weather extremes also influence pest pressures. For instance, unusually warm and humid conditions can promote diseases like coffee leaf rust (though robusta is generally more rust-resistant than arabica). Likewise, climate stress on trees can make them more vulnerable to insect infestations. While Vietnam has not faced a crisis from coffee leaf rust like some other countries, the risk is present. A changing climate that brings erratic rainfall and higher humidity could enable such diseases to spread more easily ￼. Farmers need to adapt agronomic practices to these emerging threats as well.

In summary, drought and flooding are the primary climate hazards causing year-to-year swings in Vietnamese coffee production. Drought tends to reduce yields by limiting water during critical growth phases, whereas excessive rain or storms can physically damage crops and disrupt harvests. Both extremes have been experienced in recent cycles – for example, a drought in 2022–24 followed by floods in 2025 ￼. This alternating pattern of extremes illustrates the challenges of climate variability. Other factors like heat stress, and to a lesser extent pest/disease outbreaks, also contribute to variability and are expected to grow in importance under climate change.

# Flood Monitoring and Data Availability in Vietnam

How are floods detected and monitored?  Vietnam has made significant investments in disaster monitoring. The country operates a network of meteorological stations and a national weather service (NCHMF) that monitors rainfall and river levels. Warnings for heavy rain, flash floods, and landslides are issued through this agency when extreme weather is forecast ￼. On the ground, local authorities track river heights with gauges – for example, water levels on rivers around Huế were measured reaching record highs during the 2025 floods ￼. These traditional methods are now complemented by advanced technology: satellite remote sensing plays a growing role in detecting flood events in near-real time.

Earth-observing satellites can identify inundated areas even if roads and communications are cut. Optical satellite imagery (like NASA’s MODIS on the Terra/Aqua satellites) provides frequent views that show new “dark blue” water-covered zones during floods ￼ ￼. In fact, NASA’s Earth Observatory has documented Vietnamese floods using MODIS images (for example, capturing the extent of flooding along Vietnam’s coast after storms) ￼ ￼. However, optical sensors can be hindered by cloud cover – and heavy rain events usually come with clouds. To overcome this, radar satellites such as Europe’s Sentinel-1 (Synthetic Aperture Radar) are used; radar can penetrate clouds and darkness to map water on the ground. Sentinel-1’s all-weather imaging has been successfully applied to map floods in Vietnam’s central regions, enabling emergency responders to see which villages or farms are underwater ￼.

International flood monitoring systems also provide data for Vietnam. For instance, the University of Maryland and NASA operate a Global Flood Monitoring System (GFMS) that uses satellite rainfall estimates to model flood inundation in near real-time ￼. Likewise, the Global Flood Awareness System (GloFAS) by the EU produces flood forecasts and maps using weather models and river flow simulations ￼. These tools can give advance warning of large flood events and are freely accessible, allowing Vietnamese agencies to augment their own forecasts.

Crucially, there are also public data archives that record past flood events. The Dartmouth Flood Observatory (DFO) maintains a global archive of large floods dating back to 1985, including events in Vietnam. This archive contains flood event maps and GIS data. In fact, an automated system supported by NASA/DFO has been generating daily flood inundation maps (as shapefiles) since 2011, which are available for analysis ￼. Researchers and planners can use these datasets (in GIS software or Google Earth Engine) as a “flood layer” to identify historically flooded areas and frequency. Additionally, a Global Flood Database was recently developed by scientists (Tellman et al. 2021), compiling satellite-derived flood maps for 913 major flood events worldwide from 1985–2016 – Vietnam has multiple events in that database, reflecting its recurring flooding problem. These resources mean that data on flood extent and frequency are available for Vietnam, enabling better risk assessment.

On the Vietnamese government side, the Vietnam Disaster Management Authority (under MARD) provides situation reports on floods (in Vietnamese) and likely has internal data on flood damages and areas affected ￼. However, granular flood inundation data from the government may not always be open to the public. Researchers therefore often rely on the global satellite products and academic studies for consistent data. The trend is toward more open data: for example, NASA’s SERVIR program and other NGOs have worked to build flood mapping capacity in Southeast Asia, which should improve data sharing.

In terms of historical flood context, it’s clear that flooding is a perennial issue in Vietnam, especially in the monsoon season. Over the past few decades, extreme weather events (mostly floods and storms) have caused an average of about 500 deaths per year in the country ￼. Climate change is expected to further exacerbate flood risks – through sea level rise (worsening coastal and delta floods) and through potentially stronger or more erratic rainfall events. In delta regions like the Mekong, subsidence (land sinking) and sea level rise together are creating a “hidden” flood threat, as recent satellite studies have shown the Mekong Delta sinking ~4 mm per year ￼ ￼. This means floodplains are becoming more vulnerable even without changes in rainfall. For the inland coffee regions, the main concern is intensification of the rainfall cycle – for instance, stronger La Niña phases bringing overwhelming rains, and hotter dry seasons during El Niño years bringing harsh droughts. Already, farmers have observed an oscillation of extremes: prolonged drought in 2023/24 followed by unprecedented floods in 2025 ￼. Adapting to this will require improved water management (e.g. irrigation and drainage), early warning systems, and possibly shifting cultivation practices or crop varieties.

In conclusion, Vietnamese coffee production is driven by favorable natural conditions but is highly exposed to weather and climate hazards. Flooding in the coffee heartland, once considered rare, has become a real threat as seen in recent years, while drought remains a frequent challenge. Both hazards have data available for monitoring – from satellite flood maps to rainfall records – which can be leveraged to mitigate impacts. Going forward, strengthening climate resilience (such as flood defenses, irrigation for drought, and flexible farm management) will be key to sustaining Vietnam’s coffee output in the face of these environmental risks ￼ ￼. By combining traditional knowledge with modern technology (like remote sensing and forecasting models), Vietnam is improving its ability to detect floods early and plan for disasters, which should help protect its coffee growers from the worst effects of nature’s variability.

# Factor model approach
Yes. A “factor model” is a really natural way to model Vietnamese coffee, especially if your goal is a defensible synthetic time series that responds to real-world drivers.

Below is a practical way to think about it, with options ranging from simple to sophisticated.

What you’re modeling

You usually choose one target:
	•	Yield (kg/ha) in Vietnam, robusta-dominant
	•	Production (tonnes) which is basically Yield × Area
	•	Exportable supply (production minus domestic use and carry)

For a booth-style chart, yield is often the cleanest because it links directly to weather stress.

A simple factor model structure

At its simplest:

Yield(t) = Base + Weather(t) + Agronomy(t) + Economics(t) + Noise(t)

Where each block is a small number of measurable factors.

Recommended factor groups for Vietnam coffee

Factor group	What it captures	Example variables you can actually source	Why it matters for Vietnam
Water stress	Drought risk and irrigation strain	Rainfall anomaly, soil moisture anomaly, SPEI, VHI/VCI, evapotranspiration	Central Highlands robusta is very sensitive to dry-season water availability and flowering conditions
Excess rain and flooding	Harvest disruption, cherry drop, mold risk, erosion	Extreme precipitation days, rainfall percentile, flood extent days (satellite), river level proxies	Heavy rain near harvest and extreme events can dent quality and timing even if annual rainfall looks fine
Temperature stress	Heat impacts on flowering and bean fill	Tmax anomaly, hot days above threshold, degree days	Heat amplifies drought stress and can reduce bean fill and quality
Phenology timing	Coffee’s critical windows	Lagged weather factors aligned to flowering and bean development	Coffee reacts strongly to when weather happens, not just annual totals
Area and tree age	Supply capacity and structural decline	Coffee area, replanting share, tree-age proxies	Old trees, renovation cycles, and area shifts create multi-year drifts in yield
Market incentives	Management intensity and farmer behavior	Robustas prices, input costs (fertiliser), FX (VND, USD), credit conditions	High prices can increase investment and picking intensity, low prices can reduce inputs
Shocks	“Narrative events”	Dummy variables for known drought years, flood years, policy or logistics disruptions	Helps your synthetic series reflect real discontinuities without inventing fake precision

What traders would recognise as credible drivers

If you want “this won’t look stupid to a trader,” the factors that pass the sniff test are:
	•	Drought severity in the Central Highlands (dry season water stress)
	•	Excess rainfall during harvest (harvest delays and quality)
	•	Heatwaves
	•	Export pace and farmer selling (more relevant to price than yield, but still part of the narrative)
	•	Inventory tightness (again price-facing, but it affects the “market mood”)

A concrete model you can implement quickly

Option A: Linear regression with lags (fast, explainable)

Target: annual yield or quarterly yield index

Example spec:
	•	Rain anomaly (Jun-Sep, previous year)
	•	Soil moisture anomaly (Jan-Mar)
	•	Extreme rainfall days (Oct-Dec, harvest window)
	•	Tmax anomaly (Feb-Apr)
	•	One structural trend term (tree aging or renovation proxy)
	•	One shock dummy for major drought years and major flood years

This works well for producing a plausible series even if you only have 5-10 years of data.

Option B: State-space model (nice for “forecast line” visuals)

Think of yield as:
	•	A slowly moving trend (technology, tree age, area management)
	•	Plus a weather-driven seasonal component
	•	Plus shocks

This gives you smooth “historic + near-term forecast” lines that look realistic on a chart.

Option C: Dynamic factor model (if you have many correlated climate signals)

If you have lots of climate covariates (rain, soil moisture, vegetation indices, flood extents), you can compress them into 1-3 latent factors:
	•	“Dryness factor”
	•	“Excess rain factor”
	•	“Heat factor”

Then regress yield on those factors. It’s tidy and avoids overfitting.

Data you’d typically plug in

You can do this with mostly public data:
	•	Rainfall: CHIRPS, ERA5
	•	Temperature: ERA5
	•	Soil moisture / drought indices: ERA5-Land, SPEI products
	•	Vegetation health proxies: MODIS NDVI/EVI, VHI/VCI
	•	Flood extent: Sentinel-1 flood mapping (event-based), global flood databases, or GFMS/GloFAS-derived indicators
	•	Coffee area / production: national stats, FAOSTAT, USDA PSD (depending on what you trust)

For a marketing chart, you do not need perfect measurement. You need drivers that are defensible and produce a plausible curve.

How to use it for synthetic yield from 2020 onward

If your goal is “a realistic yield line with a near-term forecast,” do this:
	1.	Pick a baseline yield level for Vietnam robusta (example: 2.6 to 3.0 t/ha as a normal band).
	2.	Convert yield to kg/ha for your axis (2.8 t/ha = 2800 kg/ha).
	3.	Build a “weather stress index” each month or quarter:
	•	Dryness increases stress
	•	Excess rain near harvest increases stress
	•	Heat adds stress
	4.	Map that stress index into yield deviations:
	•	Mild stress: -2% to -5%
	•	Bad year: -8% to -15%
	•	Extreme shock: -15% to -25% (rare, but defensible during severe drought + heat)
	5.	Add a small trend component (could be flat, slightly down if you want to signal aging trees, or slightly up if you want to signal agronomy improvement).
	6.	Forecast the next 3 months by keeping trend + seasonality and letting weather uncertainty widen the band (even if you only show a single forecast line).

Vietnam uses a mix of global, national, and local indices for flood risk assessment. These incorporate factors like hazard (e.g., flood depth/velocity), exposure (e.g., population/assets), vulnerability (e.g., socioeconomic factors), and resilience (e.g., infrastructure). Common ones include:

1. **INFORM Risk Index (Global)**: A composite index (0-10 scale) by the EU's Joint Research Centre, assessing hazard/exposure, vulnerability, and coping capacity. 2. **Think Hazard Index (Global/Regional)**: Classifies flood hazard as "high" for Vietnam (based on modeled data from GFDRR/World Bank). 3. **Flood Vulnerability Index (FVI) and Coastal Cities Flood Vulnerability Index (CCFVI)**: Indicator-based methods (e.g., from Balica et al., 2012, applied to Vietnam's Mekong Delta and coastal areas). Scores range 0-1 (low to high vulnerability):
   - Based on exposure (e.g., population density), susceptibility (e.g., poverty), and resilience (e.g., flood maps/infrastructure).

4. **Flood Risk Index (FRI)**: Used in local studies (e.g., ScienceDirect on An Giang Province). Computed as FRI = Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability (normalized 0-1 or categorical low-very high). For Mekong communes: 0.2-0.8, with high-risk areas >0.5 due to annual inundation.
